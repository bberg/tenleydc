# Brightwood Timeline

A chronological guide to the key events that shaped Brightwood, from its founding as Washington's first African American settlement through its development as a streetcar suburb and beyond.

---

## Pre-Colonial and Colonial Era

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Pre-1700s | Nacotchtank and Mattawoman peoples inhabit the region |
| 1700s | Area used primarily as farmland; Georgia Avenue exists as a toll road (7th Street Pike) connecting Brookeville, Maryland to Washington |

---

## Antebellum Period (1820s-1860)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1820s | Free African Americans establish settlement at Vinegar Hill, making Brightwood Washington's first African American settlement |
| 1832 | A.G. Pierce donates half-acre of land for Emory M.E. Church and school; original building constructed with log first floor and frame second story |
| 1837 | Documented Black family landownership around future Fort Stevens site; Black residents own at least 25 acres |
| 1840s | Name changed from Brighton to Brightwood; Archibald White and Louis Brunett credited with suggesting new name to avoid postal confusion with Maryland town |

---

## Civil War Era (1861-1865)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1861-1862 | Fort Stevens constructed as part of Civil War Defenses of Washington; built on land owned by Elizabeth Proctor Thomas ("Aunt Betty"), a free Black woman |
| 1861-1862 | Original Emory Church torn down; bricks used for fort construction and baking ovens |
| 1862 | Fort renamed from Fort Massachusetts to Fort Stevens in honor of Brigadier General Isaac Ingalls Stevens, killed at Battle of Chantilly |
| 1864 | Military Road School opens, one of first schools for African Americans in D.C. |
| July 11-12, 1864 | **Battle of Fort Stevens**: Confederate General Jubal Early attacks with approximately 20,000 soldiers; Union forces under Major General Alexander McCook repel attack |
| July 11 or 12, 1864 | President Abraham Lincoln observes battle and comes under enemy fire; becomes first and only sitting president to face hostile gunfire in office |
| July 12, 1864 | Early's forces withdraw; cross Potomac on July 13 at White's Ferry |
| 1864-1865 | Camp Brightwood serves as Union Army encampment; "contrabands" (escaped slaves) seek refuge at Union lines |

---

## Post-Civil War and Early Development (1866-1889)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1866 | Battleground National Cemetery established |
| 1870 | Stone chapel constructed at Emory Church site, led by Charles Vance |
| 1873 | Metropolitan Railroad runs horse-drawn streetcar line on Georgia Avenue to Rock Creek Church Road |
| Pre-1889 | Transportation limited to horse-drawn cart driven by African American man known as Cherry |

---

## Streetcar Era (1888-1920)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| October 18, 1888 | Congress authorizes Brightwood Railway Company to build streetcar line on Georgia Avenue from Boundary Street to District line |
| 1890 | Brightwood Railway Company purchases and electrifies Metropolitan's Georgia Avenue line; initially uses storage battery cars due to underground wiring requirements |
| March 17, 1891 | **Brightwood Citizens' Association founded**, first neighborhood association in Northwest D.C.; James L. Norris elected first president |
| 1892 | Streetcar line extended to Takoma Park |
| 1893 | Streetcars begin running on electricity via overhead lines north of Florida Avenue |
| c. 1897 | Original Brightwood School built (two-story brick building at Brightwood Avenue and Peabody Street) |
| 1899 | Streetcar line extended from Rock Creek Church Road to Brightwood |
| 1899 | Harry Wardman builds his first two speculative houses in Brightwood, launching his development career |

---

## Development Boom (1900-1945)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1905 | Walter Reed General Hospital site acquired |
| 1908-1909 | Walter Reed General Hospital opens (May 1, 1909) |
| 1909 | Brightwood Station car barn opens on Georgia Avenue; can service more than 40 streetcars |
| 1920s | Harry Wardman develops Fort Stevens Ridge subdivision (bounded by Peabody, Fifth, Underwood, and Ninth Streets); builds hundreds of semi-detached and detached homes |
| 1922 | Current Classical Revival Emory United Methodist Church completed |
| August 1925 | Construction bids accepted for new Brightwood School designed by architect Waddy B. Wood |
| September 1926 | New 16-room Brightwood School opens at 1300 Nicholson Street NW |
| 1938 | Harry Wardman dies; estimated 10% of District population lives in Wardman-constructed buildings |

---

## Civil Rights Era (1946-1970)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Late 1940s | Brightwood becomes known for Greek, Jewish, and Italian families after covenants against white ethnics broken; few African Americans in these blocks |
| May 3, 1948 | **Shelley v. Kraemer**: U.S. Supreme Court rules enforcement of racially restrictive deed covenants unconstitutional |
| 1948 | Attorney Charles Hamilton Houston wins Hurd v. Hodge, D.C. companion case to Shelley v. Kraemer |
| Early 1950s | New wave of African American families arrives following covenant rulings |
| 1954 | Brown v. Board of Education intensifies demographic transition from white to African American |
| 1950s-1960s | White flight and block-busting change neighborhood composition; Emory Church membership drops from 1,500 (1950, all white) to 30 (1976, predominantly Black) |
| 1960 | Buses replace streetcars on Georgia Avenue route (citywide conversion completed 1962); No. 70 streetcar becomes 70 bus |

---

## Modern Era (1971-Present)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| July 15, 1974 | Civil War Defenses of Washington (including Fort Stevens) listed on National Register of Historic Places |
| 2011 | Walter Reed Army Medical Center closes after over a century of operation |
| February 12, 2014 | Emory United Methodist Church added to D.C. Inventory of Historic Sites |
| August 28, 2015 | Emory United Methodist Church added to National Register of Historic Places |
| 2010s-2020s | Former Walter Reed campus redeveloped as The Parks at Walter Reed |
| Present | Brightwood maintains identity as predominantly African American neighborhood while facing gentrification pressures |

## Notes on Sources

This timeline draws primarily from research conducted for this project, including materials from the Library of Congress, DC Office of Planning, DC Historic Sites database, National Park Service, and published histories of Washington, D.C. Specific dates are cited where documentation exists; approximate dates are indicated with "c." (circa).
