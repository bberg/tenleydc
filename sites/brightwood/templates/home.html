Brightwood Almanac

History and community of Washington's first African American settlement

Brightwood stands as one of Washington, D.C.'s most historically significant neighborhoods, with roots stretching back to the 1820s when free African Americans established the area's first permanent settlement at Vinegar Hill. Located in the northwestern quadrant of the city within Ward 4, this community witnessed the only Civil War battle where a sitting president came under enemy fire.

History

In the 1820s, free African Americans began settling in the area then known as Vinegar Hill, making Brightwood Washington's first African American settlement. By 1837, Black families owned at least 25 acres of land around what would become Fort Stevens.

On July 11-12, 1864, Confederate General Jubal Early led approximately 20,000 soldiers in an attack on Fort Stevens, the only significant engagement of the Civil War fought within the District of Columbia. President Abraham Lincoln rode out from the White House to observe the battle and briefly came under enemy fire.

Transportation

Before 1889, Brightwood remained so isolated that the only transportation was a horse-drawn cart driven by an African American man known simply as Cherry. The Brightwood Railway Company, authorized in 1888, built a streetcar line on Georgia Avenue that transformed the neighborhood.
