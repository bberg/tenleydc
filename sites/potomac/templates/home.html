Potomac Almanac

History, culture, and community in Potomac, Maryland

Potomac, Maryland is an affluent unincorporated community in Montgomery County, known for its historic estates, proximity to Great Falls, and the C&O Canal. The area's rich history encompasses Native American settlements, colonial tobacco farms, and the canal era that shaped the region.

History

The Potomac area was originally home to the Piscataway and other Algonquian-speaking peoples. European settlers arrived in the early 18th century, establishing tobacco plantations along the Potomac River. The name "Potomac" derives from an Algonquian word meaning "river of swans" or "trading place."

The construction of the Chesapeake and Ohio Canal in the 1830s transformed the area, creating a vital transportation corridor for goods moving between the mountains and Georgetown. The canal operated until 1924 and is now preserved as a national historical park.

Great Falls

Great Falls of the Potomac is one of the most spectacular natural landmarks in the Washington region. The falls cascade over a series of jagged rocks, dropping 76 feet in less than a mile. The area has been a destination for visitors since the colonial era.

Modern Potomac

Today, Potomac is known as one of the wealthiest communities in the United States, with large estates, excellent schools, and a rural character that contrasts with nearby urban development. The community maintains strict zoning to preserve its open spaces and historic character.
