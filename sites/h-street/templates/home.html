H Street Almanac

Black Main Street, the fire, and the long road back

H Street NE was once Washington's second-busiest commercial district, a place where Black Washingtonians could shop without the humiliation they faced in downtown's white-owned stores. It was called Black Main Street, a thriving corridor of theaters, department stores, and local businesses serving a community denied access elsewhere.

Black Main Street

What made H Street distinctive was its role in segregated Washington. While downtown theaters and stores served Black customers reluctantly or not at all, H Street welcomed them. Black-owned businesses established themselves along the corridor. It was not a utopia of racial harmony, but it was a place where the Black community could shop with dignity.

April 1968: When H Street Burned

On April 4, 1968, Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated in Memphis. Within hours, grief and rage exploded into violence. H Street NE was looted and burned from Benning Road to the Hopscotch Bridge. What followed was not just destruction but abandonment: thirty years of disinvestment, boarded storefronts, and a community left to fend for itself.

Revival and Gentrification

Change began in the 1990s. The Atlas Theater reopened as a performing arts center in 2006. Streetcars returned in 2016. Forbes named H Street the sixth-most hipster place in America in 2012. But since 2002, over 150 businesses have been forced to close, and many long-time residents have been displaced.
