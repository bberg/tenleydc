Woodley Park Almanac

A Clearing in the Woods: Home to the National Zoo

Woodley Park takes its name from a Federal-style mansion built in 1801 by Philip Barton Key, uncle of Francis Scott Key. The name itself means "a clearing in the woods," chosen after Key visited an English manor called Woodley Lodge in Berkshire. That sense of a civilized space carved from wilderness defines a neighborhood that combines urban convenience with a distinctly green, park-like character.

History

The original Woodley Mansion served as a summer retreat for multiple U.S. presidents, including Martin Van Buren and Grover Cleveland, who sought escape from Washington's oppressive summer heat. The hilltop estate offered cooler breezes and distance from the miasmas believed to cause disease in the low-lying capital.

Development came with the completion of the Taft Bridge in 1907, which carried Connecticut Avenue over the deep ravines of Rock Creek Park. Harry Wardman, the District's most prolific developer, recognized Woodley Park's potential when others saw only inconvenient distance from downtown.

The National Zoo

The National Zoological Park, established in 1889 and designed by Frederick Law Olmsted, occupies 163 acres of Rock Creek Valley within Woodley Park's boundaries. Today it houses more than 2,100 animals and attracts millions of visitors annually, giving the neighborhood a character unlike any other in Washington.
