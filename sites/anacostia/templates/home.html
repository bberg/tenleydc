Anacostia Almanac

History and culture of Washington's first suburb

Anacostia stands as one of the most historically significant neighborhoods in Washington, D.C. Located east of the Anacostia River in Southeast Washington, this neighborhood holds a unique place in American history as both Washington's first planned suburb and the final home of Frederick Douglass.

A Tale of Two Communities

The history of Anacostia cannot be told as a single narrative. Uniontown, founded in 1854, was Washington's first planned suburb, with restrictive covenants that barred sales to Black residents. Just thirteen years later, Barry Farm-Hillsdale, established by the Freedmen's Bureau in 1867, became home to formerly enslaved people who had fled to Washington during the Civil War.

Frederick Douglass: The Sage of Anacostia

In 1877, Frederick Douglass made his most profound statement about Anacostia's racial covenants: he ignored them. The formerly enslaved man who had become an internationally known writer, lecturer, and social reformer purchased Cedar Hill for $6,700, defying the whites-only restrictions. He would live there until his death in 1895, earning the title "the Sage of Anacostia."

Civil Rights Battleground

After World War II, Barry Farm-Hillsdale became a center of civil rights activism. In 1949, youth from the community helped desegregate the Anacostia swimming pool. Parents were at the forefront of the fight to desegregate schools, facing strikes and protests in 1954.
