Southwest Waterfront Almanac

America's oldest fish market and the wound of urban renewal

The Southwest Waterfront holds a paradoxical place in Washington, D.C.'s history. It is home to the nation's oldest continuously operating fish market, established in 1805, and to some of the city's most expensive new development at The Wharf. But between these bookends lies one of the most devastating urban renewal projects in American history.

The Island

In the early 19th century, the Southwest Waterfront was one of Washington's first privately developed areas. But the Washington City Canal proved too shallow and subject to unstable tides. When railroad tracks were built along Maryland Avenue SW, the isolation was complete. The area became known as "The Island."

Urban Renewal: The Destruction

In 1950, Congress passed the District of Columbia Redevelopment Act. What followed was among the most complete demolitions of an urban neighborhood in American history. 560 acres were cleared. 99% of buildings were leveled. 23,000 residents were displaced. 1,500 businesses were destroyed.

The Fish Market Survives

The Maine Avenue Fish Market, established in 1805, is the oldest continuously operating fish market in the United States - founded 17 years before New York's Fulton Fish Market. It survived everything: wars, economic depressions, the rise and fall of the neighborhood around it.
