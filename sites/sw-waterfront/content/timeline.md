# Southwest Waterfront Historical Timeline

A chronological history of the Southwest Waterfront, from colonial origins through urban renewal and contemporary redevelopment.

## Colonial and Early Federal Era (1770s-1850)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1770s | **Carrollsburg laid out** by the Carroll family; Notley Young owns 800 acres of what is now Southwest |
| 1790s | Washington wharf hosts fish market with fishermen selling directly from boats |
| 1794-1795 | **Wheat Row constructed** - Four Late Georgian townhouses, among the oldest residential homes in D.C., first example of row houses in the capital |
| 1794-1796 | **Thomas Law House built** by Greenleaf Syndicate; Thomas Law and Elizabeth Parke Custis (Martha Washington's granddaughter) briefly reside there as "Honeymoon House" |
| 1797 | First industrial outpost (sugar refinery) built in Southwest |
| 1802 | Washington City Canal construction initiated to connect Tiber Creek with the Anacostia River |
| 1805 | **Municipal Fish Market established** - Becomes oldest continuously operating fish market in the United States |
| 1806 | St. Mary's Chapel (Barry's Chapel) dedicated - First Catholic chapel in Washington |
| 1815 | Washington City Canal opens but fails; too shallow, fills with trash, isolates Southwest as "The Island" |
| 1820s-1830s | Southwest Waterfront becomes principal commercial waterfront of the city |
| 1823 | Congress designates part of waterfront as "fish-docks," only place in D.C. where fish can legally be sold from a boat |

## Community Development Era (1850-1950)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1852 | **St. Dominic Church established** by Rev. George A.J. Wilson |
| March 19, 1854 | St. Dominic Church opens |
| November 19, 1865 | Cornerstone of current St. Dominic Church placed (months after Civil War ends) |
| Post-Civil War | Southwest becomes home to poorer classes; U.S. Colored Troops settle in area; Underground Railroad connection documented |
| 1875 | **Friendship Baptist Church organized** as First Baptist Church by Rev. R.S. Laws; St. Dominic's current building dedicated |
| 1879 | Virginia Avenue Baptist Church (later Friendship Baptist) renamed |
| 1882 | River dredging approved; materials deposited on mudflats create Potomac Park, Washington Channel, and Tidal Basin |
| 1885 | St. Dominic's Church suffers fire; Catholic and Protestant communities raise funds for restoration |
| 1886-1887 | Friendship Baptist Church building constructed by James A. Boyce |
| 1904 | Barney Neighborhood House established at Duncanson-Cranch House by artist/philanthropist Alice Barney |
| 1912 | T.W. Riley dies at age 93 after 80 years connected to fish market operation |
| 1918 | Original fish market cluster replaced by impressive block-long brick structure |
| 1919 | St. Dominic's Church consecrated |
| 1929 | St. Dominic's Church suffers second fire |
| 1940s | Jewish immigrant Al Jolson's Southwest childhood; Marvin Gaye's childhood at Fairfax Apartments |

## Urban Renewal Era (1950-1970)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1950 | **District of Columbia Redevelopment Act passed** - Establishes Redevelopment Land Agency (RLA) and National Capital Planning Commission |
| 1950 | **Arena Stage founded** by Zelda and Thomas Fichandler and Edward Mangum at Hippodrome Theatre |
| 1950 | Pre-renewal population approximately 23,500; 69-80% African American; 65% had lived there 10+ years |
| 1950s | NCPC surveys reveal: 64.3% dwellings beyond repair, 57.8% outside toilets, 60.3% no baths, 29.3% no electricity |
| 1954 | **Slum clearance begins** |
| November 22, 1954 | **Berman v. Parker** - Supreme Court rules 8-0 that private property can be taken for public purpose with just compensation |
| December 1954 | Historical Society of Washington, D.C. pressures RLA to save Wheat Row |
| 1954-1960s | **23,000 residents displaced**; **1,500 businesses displaced**; **99% of buildings demolished**; **560 acres cleared** |
| 1960 | Only **12%** of displaced households return to the quadrant |
| June 3, 1960 | Harbour Square announced - $12 million, 447-unit cooperative incorporating Wheat Row and historic buildings |
| 1960s | Fish market's 1918 structure demolished; market relocated along Washington Channel |
| October 31, 1961 | **Fichandler Stage opens** at Arena Stage, designed by Harry Weese (who also designed Washington Metro) |
| February 1963 | Harbour Square construction begins |
| 1964 | **Southwest Neighborhood Assembly founded** during displacement period |
| 1965 | Friendship Baptist congregation moves from original building |
| 1966 | **Thursz Study published** - "Where Are They Now?" documents displacement impacts; 25% of displaced make no new friends in new neighborhoods |
| 1960s-1970s | Demographic transformation: from 70% Black to 70% white |

## Post-Renewal Era (1970-2010)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| July 23, 1973 | Wheat Row added to National Register of Historic Places |
| 1973 | Thomas Law House added to National Register of Historic Places |
| 1976 | Arena Stage receives special Tony Award - First theater outside New York so honored |
| 1981 | Arena Stage develops Audio Description for visually impaired audiences |
| 2004 | Friendship Baptist Church building listed on National Register of Historic Places |
| 2008-2010 | Arena Stage renovated and expanded as Mead Center for American Theater |

## Contemporary Era (2010-Present)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| October 2017 | **The Wharf Phase I opens** - Part of $2.5 billion waterfront redevelopment |
| October 2022 | **The Wharf Phase II completed** |
| Present | Maine Avenue Fish Market continues operation, over 220 years since founding |
| Present | Wharf condominiums median list price ~$2 million; 1960s cooperatives median ~$382,500 |

## Displacement Statistics

| Measure | Number |
|---------|--------|
| Total residents displaced | 23,000+ |
| Businesses displaced | 1,500 |
| Buildings demolished | 99% of renewal area |
| Area cleared | 560 acres |
| Residents returning to quadrant (1960) | Only 12% |
| Displaced residents making no new friends | 25% |
| Pre-renewal African American population | 69-80% |
| Post-renewal African American population (20 years later) | ~30% |
| Low-income housing replaced | Only 10% |

## Buildings That Survived Urban Renewal

| Building | Original Date | Significance |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| Maine Avenue Fish Market | 1805 | Oldest continuously operating fish market in the U.S. |
| Wheat Row | 1794-1795 | Among oldest residential homes in D.C.; first row houses |
| Thomas Law House | 1794-1796 | "Honeymoon House" of Thomas Law and Martha Washington's granddaughter |
| St. Dominic's Church | 1875 | Required act of Congress to protect from demolition |
| Friendship Baptist Church (original building) | 1886-1887 | Now Blind Whino Arts Club |
| Duncanson-Cranch House | 1794 | Became Barney Neighborhood House in 1904 |
