Columbia Heights Almanac

History, culture, and community in Washington, DC

Columbia Heights stands as one of Washington, D.C.'s most dramatically transformed neighborhoods, its story arcing from colonial farmland through Gilded Age affluence, devastating upheaval, and remarkable rebirth. Located in the Northwest quadrant, this neighborhood has witnessed virtually every chapter of the American urban experience.

History

The neighborhood's most consequential early development came in 1821 when Columbian College opened on College Hill between 14th and 15th Streets, fulfilling George Washington's dream of a national university. That institution would eventually become George Washington University, relocating downtown but leaving an indelible mark on the neighborhood's identity.

The pivotal year was 1892, when the Washington and Georgetown Railroad Company began running electric streetcars up 14th Street to Park Road. This streetcar connection catalyzed Columbia Heights' metamorphosis from countryside to urban neighborhood.

Cultural Hub

The magnificent Tivoli Theatre opened on April 4, 1924, with over 2,000 seats making it Washington's largest theater. Designed in Italian Renaissance Revival style by Thomas W. Lamb, the Tivoli became the neighborhood's cultural anchor. Today it houses GALA Hispanic Theatre.

Black Broadway

Beginning in the 1940s, Columbia Heights became predominantly African American. The neighborhood, along with the U Street corridor, evolved into the heart of Black Washington, known as "Black Broadway" - a world of theaters, jazz clubs, newspapers, and civil rights organizations. Duke Ellington lived here; Marvin Gaye attended Cardozo High School.
