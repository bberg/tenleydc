Glover Park Almanac

History, culture, and community in Washington, DC

Glover Park occupies a distinctive place in Washington, D.C.'s geography and history. Perched on the heights about a half mile north of Georgetown, adjacent to the United States Naval Observatory and the Vice President's residence, this neighborhood emerged from an unlikely transformation: slaughterhouse land became a planned suburban community.

History

The neighborhood takes its name from Charles Carroll Glover (1846-1936), one of Washington's most influential banker-philanthropists. The parks he helped create represent approximately 3,200 acres, nearly half the total surface of the National Capital Parks. He played critical roles in establishing Rock Creek Park, the National Zoo, and Potomac Park.

In 1924, Glover and Anne Mills Archbold donated the land that became Glover-Archbold Park, forming the neighborhood's western boundary. The name "Glover Park" first appeared in advertisements in 1926.

An Unlikely Beginning

The neighborhood's origins involve two populations rarely associated with suburban development: free Black farmers and Georgetown's master butchers. The earliest recorded settlement appears in Georgetown tax assessments between 1808 and 1810 in the village of Wilberforce, a long-forgotten subdivision.

Holy Rood Cemetery

At the neighborhood's southern edge, Holy Rood Cemetery holds remains that connect Glover Park to the deepest currents of American history. Established in 1832, this 6.5-acre site contains approximately 7,000 burials, including as many as 1,000 free and enslaved African Americans.
