# Brightwood

![Historic view of Brightwood](/static/images/brightwood-historic.jpg)
*View of Brightwood area, early 20th century. Library of Congress.*

## Geographic and Historical Significance

Brightwood occupies a distinctive place in Washington, D.C. history as the site of the city's first African American settlement and the only Civil War battle fought within the District. Located in the northwestern quadrant of Washington within Ward 4, the neighborhood stretches north of Brightwood Park, west of Fort Totten, east of Rock Creek, and south of the Maryland line. Its rolling terrain, once covered with the gingko, chestnut, oak, and cedar trees that gave the area its name, now supports one of the city's most historically significant residential communities.

The neighborhood's story encompasses multiple intersecting narratives: free African Americans establishing landownership in the 1820s, Union soldiers defending the capital during the Civil War, streetcar companies opening the area to suburban development, and developers introducing both housing opportunities and racial restrictions. Understanding Brightwood requires grappling with all these threads and recognizing how they continue to shape the community today.

## Origins and Early Settlement (1820s-1860)

### Vinegar Hill: Washington's First African American Settlement

In the 1820s, free African Americans began settling in the area then known as Vinegar Hill, establishing what historians recognize as Washington's first African American settlement. These pioneers arrived decades before the Civil War, purchasing land and building homes in an era when Black landownership required both resources and courage.

By 1837, Black families owned at least 25 acres of land around what would become Fort Stevens. The settlement included farmers, tradesmen, and families determined to build independent lives outside the constraints of urban Washington. Among them was Elizabeth Proctor Thomas, known as "Aunt Betty," a free Black woman whose property would later be seized for military fortifications.

The name changes the area underwent tell their own story. Originally called White Mill Seat, the settlement was renamed Peter's Mill Seat, then Crystal Springs, then Brighton. In the 1840s, residents Archibald White and Louis Brunett suggested changing the name to Brightwood because the post office kept confusing Brighton with a town in Maryland. The final name reflected the area's setting amid dense woods along Rock Creek.

### Early Transportation and Isolation

Before organized transportation, Brightwood remained profoundly isolated from central Washington. The only reliable connection was a horse-drawn cart driven by an African American man known simply as Cherry. The trip was long and uncertain, and the area functioned essentially as a rural community rather than a suburb.

Georgia Avenue, the main road through the area, originated as the 7th Street Pike, a toll road connecting Brookeville, Maryland to 7th Street in downtown Washington. When the government eventually purchased this road, it would become the corridor for streetcar service that transformed the neighborhood.

### Religious and Educational Foundations

In 1832, A.G. Pierce donated a half-acre of land for a church and school, resulting in the construction of Emory M.E. Church. The original building stood two stories high: the first floor of logs served as a school, while a frame-constructed second story provided space for worship. Black worshippers were required to sit in a segregated gallery, a reminder that even in a community founded by free African Americans, the structures of white supremacy operated.

Emory Church holds the distinction of being the only Southern Methodist church in the District of Columbia founded before the Civil War. Its continuity across nearly two centuries makes it a living connection to Brightwood's earliest days.

## The Civil War Era (1861-1865)

### Fort Stevens and the Defenses of Washington

The Civil War transformed Brightwood from isolated settlement to military stronghold. As Union forces constructed a ring of fortifications around Washington, they built Fort Stevens on the high ground overlooking the approaches from the north. The fort was constructed on land owned by Elizabeth Proctor Thomas and her family, whose property was seized for military use.

Originally called Fort Massachusetts, the fortification was renamed Fort Stevens in honor of Brigadier General Isaac Ingalls Stevens, killed at the Battle of Chantilly in September 1862. The original Emory Church was torn down during construction, its bricks repurposed for the fort and for baking ovens to feed the garrison.

### The Battle of Fort Stevens (July 11-12, 1864)

On July 11, 1864, Confederate Lieutenant General Jubal A. Early led approximately 20,000 soldiers in an attack on Fort Stevens, the only significant military engagement of the Civil War fought within the District of Columbia. The fort stood just four miles from the White House, and the attack created genuine panic in the capital.

Union forces under Major General Alexander McD. McCook successfully repulsed the Confederate assault over two days of fighting. The engagement was not a major battle by Civil War standards, but its location gave it outsized significance.

### Lincoln Under Fire

President Abraham Lincoln, accompanied by his wife Mary, rode out from the White House to observe the fighting on July 11 or 12. Standing on the parapet of the fort to get a better view, Lincoln came briefly under enemy fire that wounded a Union surgeon standing next to him.

An officer, possibly Horatio Wright or, according to legend, future Supreme Court Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr., brusquely ordered the president to take cover. Lincoln complied, but not before becoming the first and only sitting president to come under direct and purposeful fire from an enemy soldier while in office.

Early's forces withdrew on the evening of July 12, crossing the Potomac River on July 13 at White's Ferry into Leesburg, Virginia. Early reportedly remarked afterward that while they had not taken Washington, they had certainly frightened the president.

### Camp Brightwood and Contraband Refugees

During the war, Camp Brightwood served as a Union Army encampment. The Library of Congress holds photographs from this period showing "contrabands," the term used for enslaved people who escaped to Union lines, at Camp Brightwood with the 2nd Rhode Island Volunteer Infantry.

These refugees represented a different path to Brightwood than the free African Americans who had settled at Vinegar Hill. Many would remain after the war, joining the established community and becoming homeowners themselves. The convergence of these populations, free Blacks and formerly enslaved people, created the foundation for Brightwood's African American community.

### Military Road School

In 1864, even as battle raged at Fort Stevens, the Military Road School opened as one of the first schools established for African Americans in the District of Columbia. The school represented the community's determination to educate their children during a time of extraordinary crisis and uncertainty. Its founding during wartime speaks to the priorities of Brightwood's African American residents: even amid the chaos of war, education remained essential.

## The Streetcar Era (1873-1920)

### Horse-Drawn Beginnings

The Metropolitan Railroad Company ran a horse-drawn streetcar line on Georgia Avenue to Rock Creek Church Road beginning around 1873. The service was slow and unreliable, but it represented the first regular connection between Brightwood and the growing city to the south.

### The Brightwood Railway Company

On October 18, 1888, Congress authorized the Brightwood Railway Company to build a streetcar line on Georgia Avenue from Boundary Street to the District line at Silver Spring. Four of the five company founders were partners in the Petworth subdivision, including the line's president, A.A. Thomas. This overlap between transportation and real estate development would prove typical of Washington's streetcar era.

The Brightwood Railway Company purchased the Metropolitan's Georgia Avenue line in 1890 and electrified it. Because all wiring within the city was required to be underground, the line initially used storage battery cars. By 1893, the company had installed overhead electric lines north of Florida Avenue, where underground requirements did not apply. Southbound streetcars had to stop at Florida Avenue to switch from overhead lines to underground conduit.

The streetcar line earned a reputation for unreliability that became part of local folklore. The cars frequently came off their tracks, and passengers were often asked to help push them back on. Residents dubbed it the "G.O.P. (Get Out and Push) line," a nickname that captured both the frustration and the humor of early transit.

### The First Civic Association

On March 17, 1891, residents founded the Brightwood Citizens' Association, the first neighborhood association established anywhere in Northwest D.C. James L. Norris served as the organization's first president. The association's primary initial purpose was advocating for more reliable transportation, reflecting how central the streetcar had become to community life.

### Extending the Line

The streetcar line was extended to Takoma Park in 1892 and from Rock Creek Church Road to Brightwood in 1899. Each extension opened new areas to development and drew Brightwood more firmly into Washington's urban orbit. In 1909, the Brightwood Station car barn opened on Georgia Avenue, capable of servicing more than 40 streetcars at once.

## The Development Boom (1899-1945)

### Harry Wardman's Vision

Harry Wardman, who would become the District's most prolific developer, made his first foray into real estate in 1899 by building two speculative frame houses in Brightwood. This modest beginning launched a career that would reshape Washington's residential landscape.

In the 1920s, Wardman purchased a large tract bounded by Peabody Street, Fifth Street, Underwood Street, and Ninth Street. He named it Fort Stevens Ridge and developed hundreds of semi-detached and detached homes featuring covered porches and yard space. These houses, built for middle-class families seeking suburban amenities within the city, established the residential character that defines much of Brightwood today.

By Wardman's death in 1938, an estimated ten percent of the District's population lived in buildings he had constructed. His impact on Brightwood was representative of his broader influence on Washington.

### Architectural Character

The homes Wardman and other developers built in Brightwood represented several architectural styles popular in the early twentieth century:

- Rowhouses with shared walls, maximizing land use while providing individual homes
- Semi-detached houses sharing a single wall with a neighbor
- Detached single-family homes with front and side yards
- Wardman-style flats, small apartment buildings that blended into residential streetscapes

The architecture reflected the aspirations of the middle-class families who moved to Brightwood: traditional styles suggesting stability and permanence, porches for sociability, and enough space for gardens and children to play.

### Walter Reed Army Medical Center

The need for a dedicated Army hospital dated back to the Civil War, when Surgeon General William A. Hammond first conceived of the idea in 1862. Lieutenant Colonel William Cline Borden revived the proposal following the death of his friend Walter Reed in 1902.

The original site was acquired in 1905, and the central hospital building was designed by architects Marsh and Peter in the Colonial/Georgian Revival style. Walter Reed General Hospital opened on May 1, 1909, becoming a major employer and economic anchor for upper Ward 4 communities.

For over a century, Walter Reed shaped the surrounding neighborhoods, including Brightwood, Shepherd Park, Takoma, Colonial Village, and North Portal Estates. Medical staff, support workers, and their families became part of the community fabric. The hospital's closure in 2011 marked the end of an era; the campus is now being redeveloped as The Parks at Walter Reed.

### Schools and Churches

The Brightwood Education Campus traces its history to the original Brightwood School, a two-story brick building constructed around 1897 at Brightwood Avenue (now Georgia Avenue) and Peabody Street. Architect Waddy B. Wood designed the replacement school, with construction bids accepted in August 1925 and the new 16-room school opening in September 1926.

Emory United Methodist Church, after decades in its 1870 stone chapel, completed its current Classical Revival building in 1922. The new church featured a pediment portico, dentilled cornice and frieze, massive Doric columns, granite walls, and terracotta trim. This building was added to the National Register of Historic Places on August 28, 2015.

## Demographic and Social History

### Racial Covenants and Exclusion

When developers like Harry Wardman built in Brightwood, they frequently included racial covenants in property deeds, legally requiring that homes be occupied solely by white residents. Many covenants also excluded Jews and other "Semitic groups."

These restrictions created a bitter irony: a neighborhood founded by free African Americans in the 1820s was developed a century later with legal barriers preventing Black residents from purchasing new homes. The covenants reflected the systematic racism embedded in real estate practices throughout Washington and the nation.

### The Fight Against Covenants

On May 3, 1948, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in Shelley v. Kraemer that enforcement of racially restrictive deed covenants was unconstitutional. Attorney Charles Hamilton Houston argued and won Hurd v. Hodge, the D.C. companion case that applied the same principle to the District of Columbia.

This legal victory did not immediately transform Brightwood, but it removed the judicial enforcement mechanism that had maintained segregation. By the late 1940s, Brightwood had become known for its Greek, Jewish, and Italian families after covenants against white ethnics were broken. Yet African Americans remained largely excluded from these blocks.

### Integration and Transition

A new wave of African American families arrived in the early 1950s following the covenant rulings. The 1954 Brown v. Board of Education decision ending school segregation intensified the demographic transition.

Some white families, fearing racial change, moved away. Others were targeted by block-busting real estate agents who used racist fears to drive sales. Still others defied these pressures and stayed. The African American families who joined them came seeking the same things many longtime residents valued: attractive houses convenient to stores, schools, and transportation.

The pace of change can be measured in church membership. Emory Methodist Church, which had 1,500 members in 1950, all white, had dwindled to 30 people by 1976, predominantly Black. The building remained; the congregation transformed.

## The Neighborhood Today

### Physical Character

Contemporary Brightwood retains the residential character established during the development boom of the 1920s and 1930s. Tree-lined streets are bordered by rowhouses, semi-detached homes, and detached single-family houses. Front porches remain common, and many blocks maintain a cohesive architectural vocabulary.

Georgia Avenue serves as the primary commercial corridor, with local businesses, restaurants, and services. The Kennedy Street commercial district has experienced recent revitalization, including the reopening of venues honoring the area's mid-century entertainment history.

### Demographics and Community

Brightwood remains a predominantly African American neighborhood, maintaining continuity with its founding nearly two centuries ago. The community includes longtime residents whose families have lived in the area for generations alongside newer arrivals drawn by housing costs lower than many other Northwest neighborhoods.

### The Parks at Walter Reed

The former Walter Reed Army Medical Center campus, which closed in 2011, is undergoing transformation as The Parks at Walter Reed. The mixed-use development preserves historic structures while adding housing, retail, and community facilities. This redevelopment represents both an opportunity for the neighborhood and a source of concern about potential displacement and gentrification.

### Preservation and Change

Brightwood faces the tension common to many historically African American neighborhoods: development pressure that can increase property values and improve services but also displace longtime residents. Community organizations advocate for inclusive development that honors the neighborhood's history while addressing contemporary needs.

## Historic Sites and Landmarks

### Fort Stevens

The partially preserved remains of Fort Stevens, maintained by the National Park Service, mark the site of the only Civil War battle fought within Washington, D.C. Visitors can walk the grounds where President Lincoln came under fire and view interpretive markers explaining the 1864 engagement.

### Battleground National Cemetery

Located at 6625 Georgia Avenue NW, Battleground National Cemetery is one of the nation's smallest national cemeteries, containing just one acre. The cemetery holds the graves of 40 Union soldiers killed at the Battle of Fort Stevens, with 41 total headstones. Uniquely among Civil War cemeteries, all the dead are identified; there are no unknown soldiers.

The last burial took place in March 1936 when Edward R. Campbell, age 92, was interred. The cemetery was listed on the National Register of Historic Places on October 15, 1966.

### Emory United Methodist Church

The Classical Revival church building completed in 1922 stands as a landmark on Georgia Avenue. Its massive Doric columns and granite walls represent the congregation's investment in permanence after nearly a century of worship in more modest structures. The building was listed on the National Register of Historic Places on August 28, 2015.

### Brightwood Station Car Barn

The car barn that opened in 1909 to service the Brightwood Railway's streetcars could accommodate more than 40 cars at once. After buses replaced streetcars in 1960, the building housed auto dealerships. It remains a physical reminder of the streetcar era that transformed the neighborhood.

## Connection to the Larger Region

Brightwood's history connects to broader themes that shaped Washington and the nation. The African American settlement at Vinegar Hill exemplified the determination of free Black people to establish independent communities in the antebellum era. The Battle of Fort Stevens represented the Civil War's reach into the heart of the Union. The streetcar lines that enabled development reflected transportation's role in creating suburban communities. And the story of racial covenants and their eventual defeat illuminates both the mechanisms of segregation and the long struggle against them.

The neighborhood's transit connections, particularly the Georgia Avenue bus routes (70, 79) and proximity to Takoma and Fort Totten Metro stations, continue to link Brightwood to the larger metropolitan area while maintaining its distinct identity as a community with roots stretching back nearly two hundred years.

## Walking the Neighborhood

A walk through Brightwood offers opportunities to encounter history at multiple scales. The preserved sections of Fort Stevens and the graves at Battleground National Cemetery provide direct connections to the Civil War. Emory United Methodist Church represents nearly two centuries of continuous worship. The residential streets developed by Wardman and others display early twentieth-century architecture designed for middle-class family life.

The ordinary streetscape, rowhouses with their front porches, small front gardens, and neighborhood businesses, tells the story of generations of families making lives in this community: the free African Americans who first settled at Vinegar Hill, the Union soldiers who defended the capital, the diverse families who built community in the streetcar era, and the contemporary residents who maintain Brightwood's traditions while facing new challenges.
