# Columbia Heights Timeline

## Early History and Development

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1815 | William J. Stone purchases 121-acre tract of the Holmead estate, establishing Stone Farm |
| 1821 | Columbian College (now George Washington University) opens on College Hill between 14th and 15th Streets |
| 1822 | Construction of first building for Columbian College completed |
| 1860s | Horse-drawn streetcars begin connecting the neighborhood to downtown Washington after the Civil War |
| 1880s | Northern portion of modern Columbia Heights separates from the village of Mount Pleasant |

## The Streetcar Era

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1892 | Washington and Georgetown Railroad Company begins electric streetcar service up 14th Street to Park Road |
| 1892 | Romanesque car barn constructed on west side of 14th Street |
| 1898 | The Olympia Apartments built, the only surviving apartment from the 1890s streetcar boom |
| 1902 | Major building boom begins with streetcar expansion along 11th, 14th, and 16th Streets |
| 1904 | Columbia Heights Citizens' Association publishes promotional brochure touting neighborhood advantages |
| 1905 | Inter-American Defense Board Headquarters (2600 16th Street) constructed as part of Mary Foote Henderson's plan |
| 1907 | Streetcar line extended; investors including Emile Berliner transform the car barn into the Arcade entertainment complex |

## Golden Age

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1914 | Four streetcar lines serve Columbia Heights, providing 20-minute service to downtown |
| 1916 | Central Senior High School building constructed at 13th and Clifton Streets |
| Early 1920s | Duke Ellington lives in Columbia Heights |
| 1921 | All Souls Church, Unitarian founded at 6th and D Streets NW relocates |
| January 28, 1922 | Knickerbocker Theater disaster kills 98, injures 136; leads to new theater safety standards |
| 1922-1924 | All Souls Church completes current red brick Colonial Revival building on 16th Street |
| April 4, 1924 | Tivoli Theatre opens with parade from Pennsylvania Avenue; 2,000+ seats make it DC's largest theater |
| 1925 | First Lady Grace Coolidge attends events at the Tivoli Theatre |
| 1928 | Warner Bros. begins operating the Tivoli Theatre |

## Transformation and Civil Rights Era

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1934 | Banneker Community Center built; later listed on National Register (1986) |
| 1937 | All Souls Church hosts Police Boys Club No. 10 (since 1937) |
| 1940s | Demographic shift begins as Cardozo High School designation draws African American families |
| 1948 | Central High School significantly under-enrolled; Black Cardozo High School severely overcrowded (1,800 in space for 1,040) |
| 1949 | Congressional study recommends closing Cardozo; activists begin "Central for Cardozo" campaign |
| 1950-51 | Cardozo moves into the Central High School building after 10,000-signature petition |
| 1954 | Brown v. Board of Education leads to school integration; All Souls forms Columbia Heights Boys Club after Police Boys Club refuses to integrate |
| 1954 | Reverend A. Powell Davies leads civil rights activism at All Souls Church (1944-1957) |
| 1965 | James Reeb, Assistant Minister at All Souls, murdered in Selma, Alabama |
| 1965 | District Theaters takes over Tivoli Theatre operations |
| 1967 | Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. speaks to 3,800 at Cardozo Stadium supporting community-controlled urban renewal |
| May 1968 | National Welfare Rights Organization Mothers Day march concludes at Cardozo |

## The 1968 Riots and Aftermath

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| April 4, 1968 | Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. assassinated in Memphis; crowds gather at 14th and U Streets NW |
| April 1968 | More than 275 businesses along 14th Street damaged; half are total losses; 12 people die |
| April 1968 | 13,000 troops arrive in Washington, most to occupy an American city since the Civil War |
| 1968-1999 | Large portions of homes and businesses remain empty for nearly 30 years |
| 1976 | Tivoli Theatre closes |
| 1985 | Tivoli Theatre listed on the National Register of Historic Places |

## Recovery and Renaissance

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1984 | Development Corporation of Columbia Heights (DCCH) founded by nine community activists |
| 1991 | U Street Metro station opens, beginning economic recovery |
| 1999 | Columbia Heights Metro station opens as catalyst for redevelopment |
| 1999 | City announces revitalization initiative focused around Metro station |
| 1999-2005 | Tivoli Theatre undergoes six-year renovation |
| 2005 | Tivoli Theatre reopens as home of GALA Hispanic Theatre |
| 2009 | Mayor Adrian Fenty signs DC Marriage Equality Act at All Souls Church sanctuary |
| 2012 | Columbia Heights named one of fastest-gentrifying neighborhoods in the United States |
| 2020 | All Souls Church, Unitarian listed on National Register of Historic Places |

## Key Dates Summary

- **1821**: Columbian College founded
- **1892**: Electric streetcar transforms neighborhood accessibility
- **1924**: Tivoli Theatre and All Souls Church completed
- **1950**: Cardozo moves to Central building after community campaign
- **1968**: Civil uprisings devastate commercial corridor
- **1999**: Metro opens, sparking renaissance
- **2005**: Tivoli reopens as GALA Hispanic Theatre
