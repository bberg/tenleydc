# Kalorama Timeline

A chronological guide to the key events that shaped Kalorama, from the original manor house through its transformation into one of Washington's most prestigious neighborhoods and diplomatic enclaves.

---

## Pre-Colonial and Colonial Era

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Pre-1600s | Nacotchtank and Mattawoman tribes inhabit the region |
| 1600s | Land grants issued by Charles II of England; tracts split and deeded over time |
| 1700s | Area becomes part of a land tract named "Widow's Mite" |

---

## The Original Estate (1750-1806)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| c. 1750 | **Anthony Holmead**, Scottish immigrant and early Washington proprietor, builds original manor house with bricks imported from England |
| 1795 | Holmead builds new house "Rock Hill" for himself; sells first house to City Commissioner **Gustavus Scott** |
| 1795 | Scott adds gardens and landscaping; names estate **"Belair"** |
| 1803 | Gustavus Scott dies |
| c. 1803-1807 | **William Augustine Washington**, nephew of George Washington, owns the property briefly |

---

## Joel Barlow's Kalorama (1807-1820)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1807 | Poet and diplomat **Joel Barlow** purchases the estate at suggestion of President Thomas Jefferson |
| 1807 | Barlow renames estate **"Kalorama"** (Greek for "beautiful view"), feeling Belair too common a name |
| 1807-1812 | Barlow improves estate; builds second house on highest portion of land |
| 1807-1812 | Architect **Benjamin Henry Latrobe** renovates and expands original house |
| 1807-1812 | Barlow hosts President Thomas Jefferson and inventor Robert Fulton at Kalorama |
| 1812 | Joel Barlow dies |
| 1820 | **Susan Decatur**, widow of Commodore Stephen Decatur, moves to Kalorama with Decatur nieces |
| 1820 | Remains of Stephen Decatur temporarily placed in Barlow family mausoleum |

---

## Civil War and Early Development (1861-1890)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1861-1865 | Civil War: Grand hilltop residences turned over to army for use as barracks and hospitals |
| 1880s | Kalorama estate sold and subdivided into lots |
| 1888-1930 | Major building period in Kalorama Triangle |

---

## Streetcar Era and Development Boom (1891-1930)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1891 | Rock Creek gorge first bridged at site of future Duke Ellington Bridge |
| 1897 | Columbia Road streetcar line opens, enabling rapid development |
| 1902-1905 | Over 100 rowhouses constructed in Kalorama Triangle |
| 1906 | **Taft Bridge** completed, carrying Connecticut Avenue over Rock Creek gorge |
| 1906-1907 | **Codman-Davis House** (The Louise Home) designed by architect Ogden Codman at 2145 Decatur Place |
| 1907 | **Charles Evans Hughes House** built at 2223 R Street (now Chancery of Burma); designed by George Oakley Totten |
| 1911 | **Spanish Steps** (Decatur Terrace Steps and Fountain) constructed at 22nd Street NW; designed by Robert E. Cook |
| 1911 | **The Brighton** apartment building completed at 2123 California Street NW (seven-story Beaux-Arts) |
| 1915 | **Woodrow Wilson House** built at 2340 S Street NW by Henry Fairbanks; designed by architect Waddy Wood |
| 1919 | **Kalorama Citizens Association** founded |

---

## The Wilson Era and Early Embassy Period (1920-1940)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| December 1920 | President Woodrow Wilson purchases S Street house as gift to wife Edith Bolling Wilson |
| March 4, 1921 | Wilsons move into 2340 S Street, the day Warren G. Harding is inaugurated |
| 1921 | **Phillips Collection** opens at 1600 21st Street NW; America's first museum of modern art |
| 1923 | Phillips Collection acquires Renoir's *Luncheon of the Boating Party* |
| February 3, 1924 | Woodrow Wilson dies in upstairs bedroom of S Street house |
| 1930s | **Great Depression**: Many mansions sold to foreign governments; transformation to Embassy Row begins |
| 1930s | **France** becomes first nation to purchase property for embassy/residence on S Street |
| 1930s | **Embassy of Siam** (Thailand) becomes first purpose-built embassy in neighborhood |
| 1933-1935 | **Calvert Street Bridge** (later Duke Ellington Bridge) constructed; designed by Paul Philippe Cret |

---

## Modern Era (1948-Present)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May 3, 1948 | Supreme Court rules in **Shelley v. Kraemer** that enforcement of racial covenants is unconstitutional |
| 1948 | Charles Hamilton Houston wins **Hurd v. Hodge**, D.C. companion case |
| 1961 | Edith Bolling Wilson dies; bequeaths S Street house to National Trust for Historic Preservation |
| 1964 | Woodrow Wilson House designated **National Historic Landmark** |
| 1974 | Calvert Street Bridge renamed **Duke Ellington Bridge** |
| April 27, 1987 | **Kalorama Triangle Historic District** listed on DC Inventory of Historic Sites |
| May 4, 1987 | Kalorama Triangle Historic District listed on National Register of Historic Places |
| 1989 | **Sheridan-Kalorama Historic District** designated (610 buildings) |
| 1999 | Spanish Steps restored after erosion and collision damage |
| January 2017 | Former President **Barack Obama** moves to Kalorama with family after leaving presidency |
| 2017 | **Jeff Bezos** purchases $23 million Kalorama house (former Textile Museum), largest private residence in D.C. |
